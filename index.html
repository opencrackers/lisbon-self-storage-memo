Investment memo · Greater Lisbon · May 2026

Self-storage looks possible, but only with a cheap conversion site.

For a 2,000sqm+ first site around Cascais/Sintra or Almada/Caparica, the attractive play is not a generic land purchase. It is an underpriced warehouse or light-industrial conversion with clean use permissions, visible road access, and enough catchment demand to reach 80%+ occupancy without heavy staffing.

Base verdict: pursue only if all-in project cost lands below roughly €1,500 per gross building sqm for conversion. At 65% net-rentable efficiency and 50% debt, that means roughly €1,150 of equity per net rentable sqm. Above that basis, the yield gets too close to industrial real-estate returns while you still carry operating, marketing, licensing, and ramp-up risk.

Critical Thesis

Where I see opportunity

  • Cascais town / Abóboda / Alcabideche / São Domingos de Rana: strong household wealth, expat/renovation/seasonal-home demand, and a clearer convenience gap than the already-served inner Cascais-line corridor.
  • Specific Sintra micro-catchments near IC19/A16: cheaper industrial stock, dense population, small-business demand, but Bluespace already operates in Massamá, so the opportunity has to be site-specific rather than a broad "Sintra is empty" thesis.
  • Caparica/Monte de Caparica: possible gap for residents, seasonal operators, surf/tourism businesses, students, and small trades. Needs very careful competition check because Almada/Cacilhas is already served.

Where I would be careful

  • Buying land to build new: likely too slow and uncertain for a first site. Planning time and construction cost can erase the benefit of operating leverage.
  • Prime industrial warehouses: if the building is good enough for logistics users, you will compete with logistics rents and yields. Self-storage only wins if retail storage rent materially beats industrial rent after conversion cost.
  • Central Lisbon logic applied to suburbs: online competitor prices look high, but large units and discounting pull the blended rate down.

Go condition

All-in cost below €1,500/gross sqm, zoning/use class already compatible with storage, fire-safety route clear, and at least 1,300sqm net rentable from a 2,000sqm building.

No-go condition

Warehouse acquisition above €1,600-1,800/gross sqm before fit-out, weak road visibility, no 24/7 access, or any need for a speculative use-class battle with the municipality.

Best first move

Lease-option or purchase-option a neglected warehouse for 6-12 months while you pre-sell units, validate search demand, and complete licensing/fire due diligence.

Market Logic

Demand drivers around Lisbon

  • Expensive housing and smaller flats create persistent overflow storage demand.
  • Renovations, moving, separations, expat relocation, and seasonal property use create short-term consumer demand.
  • E-commerce sellers, trades, restaurants, events, and tourism operators create small-business demand.
  • Suburban car access matters more than central foot traffic for large units.

Supply signal

Lisbon already has professional operators, and the coverage is less thin than a first pass suggests. Bluespace now shows seven Lisbon-area centres, including Massamá in Sintra municipality and Carnaxide/Alfragide on the Lisbon-Cascais corridor. Cascais town proper still looks more interesting than the corridor, while Almada is more competitive, with Allstorage and Stokarea already advertising south-bank coverage.

Interpretation: the gap is not "Lisbon has no storage" or even "Sintra/Cascais line is empty". The gap, if it exists, is in very specific micro-catchments with convenient access, enough population, low direct supply, and credible pricing.

Demand sizing test

The missing diligence item is supply per capita inside the real catchment, usually a 3-5 km drive radius or a 10-15 minute drive-time polygon. For any candidate site, calculate:

absorbable demand = catchment population × target rentable sqm per capita − existing competitor rentable sqm in that catchment.

Example screen: a 100,000-person catchment at 0.02 sqm/capita implies 2,000sqm of market demand before subtracting existing centres. If mapped competitor supply inside the catchment is already 1,200sqm, the residual is only 800sqm and a 1,300sqm net-rentable first site needs a stronger business-customer angle or wider draw. This is the number that defends, or kills, the 80-85% stabilized occupancy assumption.

FEDESSA/CBRE's 2025 European benchmark, roughly €312.56/sqm/year average rent and about 81% average occupancy, supports the €23/sqm/month default as conservative for a suburban Portuguese site, but it does not replace catchment-level demand sizing.

International Operating Models

US REIT model

Public Storage and Extra Space scale through ownership, acquisitions, development, revenue management, national marketing, and ancillary income. Public Storage's 2025 average same-store occupancy was around 92%, and its same-store NOI margin was about 78% in late-2025 reporting.

Mature margin benchmark: high, but not realistic for a first Portugal site.

European operator model

Shurgard, Bluespace and similar operators pursue dense urban/suburban catchments, premium security, web-led acquisition, revenue management, and either freehold or long leasehold property. Europe is less penetrated than the US, so growth runway exists.

Likely target margin for a small Portuguese site: 55-65% NOI when stabilized.

Low-staff automation model

Online booking, remote KYC, smart locks/access code, CCTV, alarms, digital contracts, card billing, and call-centre support. This suits Portugal if the first site is simple and customers can access units without staff.

Best fit for Julian: operationally lean, but front-loaded capex and tech discipline matter.

How they finance

Large operators use a mix of retained cash flow, bank debt, bonds, equity issuance, joint ventures, and acquisitions. Small operators usually use bank leverage against the property plus founder equity. For Portugal today, underwriting should assume Euribor-linked debt around 5-7% all-in, with lower leverage until the bank sees proven cash flow. Key the floating-rate case to the tenor the bank actually uses, usually 3M or 6M Euribor rather than 12M. A new site may get 50-60% loan-to-cost; a stabilized property might refinance higher if DSCR is strong.

Portugal-Specific Risk Assessment

Licensing and fire safety

Self-storage sits close to warehouse/storage use, but conversion can trigger municipal use-class, building-works, accessibility, and SCIE/fire-safety requirements. This is the first diligence gate.

Acquisition price risk

Greater Lisbon industrial stock is tight. If logistics users can pay €4-7/sqm/month rent or buyers pay €800-1,500/sqm, the storage project must beat that alternative use. A commercial acquisition also needs roughly 6.5% IMT plus 0.8% stamp duty included in the basis gate.

Ramp-up risk

The business may look bad for 24-36 months. Debt service starts immediately; occupancy does not. Initial leverage should be sized for the lease-up burn, not the stabilized DSCR.

VAT treatment

Self-storage in Portugal is generally a VATable service at the standard 23% rate. That can allow input VAT recovery on fit-out capex, but consumer customers cannot reclaim VAT on rent, while many business customers can. This pushes the model toward a sharper customer-mix view.

Marketing risk

Operators increasingly depend on website, search, reviews, dynamic pricing, and conversion tracking. A hidden industrial site with weak SEO will not fill just because storage demand exists.

Security and insurance

24/7 access needs CCTV, individual alarms/locks, access logs, insurance structure, prohibited-goods clauses, and robust default/auction process.

Customer mix

Consumers give higher €/sqm but churn more. Businesses take larger spaces at lower €/sqm but can stabilise occupancy. The target mix should be 55-65% consumer, 35-45% business.

Exit value

The conversion only creates value if stabilized yield-on-cost clears the eventual self-storage exit cap rate by enough to pay for lease-up burn, reserves, and execution risk. A thin spread is not worth the operating complexity.

What Would Be Needed

Deal-screening checklist

  • 2,000-4,000sqm gross building or expandable industrial site.
  • At least 60-70% conversion to net rentable units; underwrite the stress case at 60%, not only the 65% target.
  • Direct van access, loading, parking, 24/7 gate possibility.
  • Dry, secure, no major roof/flood/humidity problem.
  • Existing warehouse/industrial/commercial use, not residential grey zone.
  • All-in cost below €1,500/gross sqm after works, IMT, stamp duty, soft costs, and VAT treatment.

Validation before offer

  • Call 8-12 competitors as a customer for real quotes, discounts, availability, deposits, and waiting lists.
  • Map every competitor within 3-5 km drive radius, estimate rentable sqm supply, and calculate supply per capita before underwriting 80-85% occupancy.
  • Run Google Ads test landing pages for Cascais town, Alcabideche/Abóboda, Caparica, and only specific Sintra micro-catchments to measure lead cost.
  • Ask architect/fire engineer for a fast SCIE/use-class memo on each candidate site.
  • Get bank term sheets under three cases: 50%, 60%, and 65% LTC.
  • Size equity for 36 months of lease-up burn plus operating/debt-service reserves before calculating cash-on-cash.
  • Pre-sell business units to trades/e-commerce/tourism operators before closing.

Final recommendation

I would not start by buying raw land. I would hunt for a mispriced warehouse/factory in Cascais town/Alcabideche/Abóboda or a proven under-supplied Sintra micro-catchment, preferably with an option structure, and validate demand before committing full capex. Caparica is interesting only if the site is cheap and you can prove a local underserved customer base; central Almada is already too contested unless the building is exceptional.

The opportunity exists, but it is a real-estate acquisition game first and an operating business second. The winner will be whoever buys the right box at the right basis.
